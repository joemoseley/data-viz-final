An Analysis of Title 1 Eligible Schools in Chicago

by: Joseph Moseley

Title 1 is a federally funded educational program providing extra funds to schools that enroll a large percentage of low income students. Schools enrolling over 40% low income student are eligible for these extra funds. Percentage of low income students is determined by the number of students enrolled in the free lunch and reduced lunch programs. The funding is designed to help schools meet the educational goals of low income students.

Map of High Schools

This map shows title 1 eligible high schools in the city with informational tooltips showing total enrollent, percentage of low income students, and average performance on standardized tests. There are fewer high schools because they are able to reach many more neighborhoods and have fewer low income students. Usually giving them enough to not qualify for Title 1 funding.

Map of Elementary Schools

Simmilar to the above map, this map shows the same information for Title 1 eligible elementary schools. There are many more elementary schools because of lower enrollment and most elementary schools are neighborhood schools.

The vast majority of students in Title 1 Eligible schools are black or hispanic. This is expectedly consistent with child poverty demographics in the city. Child poverty rate in recent years have been reported to be as high as 35% in the city, 17% in suburban cook county, and 13.1% in the surrounding suburban counties.

Poverty vs. Performance

This scatter plot compares the percentage of low income students in a school to thier average performance percentile on standardized tests.

This chart shows that school performance is somewhat linked to household income. A large concentration of schools with a large amount of low income students have low test scores, and as the score begin to improve, the number of low income students begins to decrease.
